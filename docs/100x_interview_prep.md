# 100x Impact - conversation prep

Nick has told us how he thinks and handed us the questions, so this is guidance to hold, not a script to recite. The frame is his: fit and collaboration, not skills - he's conceded everyone shortlisted can do the work. So the goal is to be the people he wants in the room for six months. Warm, unguarded, thinking out loud, comfortable disagreeing with each other. Over-polished loses this one. Disagreeing with him thoughtfully is worth more than a polished answer.

Two things throughout. Agree in advance who leads what - Jack on the strategy, Working Group and fit answers, Max on risk, prototyping and the AI-limits answer - but both stay live and step in unprompted, because if Max is quiet the "no handoffs, both founders do the work" story rings hollow. And there's no presentation and it's just Nick, so treat it as a conversation.

## The thing behind the brief

Nick wrote that he's assessing fit for "further phases of work in the future". That suggests 100x is thinking about a build partner after the review, and it is probably why the practitioner positioning got us shortlisted. The trap is the mirror image: if the review looks like a sales funnel for our own build work, the vendor-neutrality claim collapses. Hold both openly, and say it unprompted if the moment arises - we would like to build what comes out of this, and precisely because of that we will be disciplined about recommending things we would not be the ones to build.

## General questions

**Biggest risk not in the brief.** Don't say stakeholder availability - it's already in our proposal and it's what everyone will say. The real risk is the report-to-implementation gap: a small team under growth pressure treats this as a systems project, and it ends up owned by nobody once we leave. A good report that doesn't change how people work. That's why Phase 3 embeds the Working Group as the future governance body rather than an audience. Hold that one with conviction and keep the LSE systems boundary in reserve - if LSE's IT environment constrains architecture and AI tooling choices, part of the roadmap could be undeliverable, which is why we probe it in month one, not month five. The reserve option doubles as pre-empting the LSE question.

**What decisions should the senior team be able to make in six months that they can't today.** Answer in their language: whether to build or buy, and roughly what it costs; which AI uses to sanction and which to prohibit, and on what governance basis; who owns which data and what the stewardship model is; what capability to hire or grow internally. The frame to land - today those are guesses made in people's heads, in six months they are decisions with evidence and price tags attached. Tie back to ventures: better decisions here mean less time wrestling systems and more time on the portfolio.

**How we'd work with the Data & AI Working Group.** His favourite theme, so answer with the instinct, not a process diagram. They are co-analysts, not an audience - involved from inception, testing findings before they are findings, and handed the governance mandate at the closing session. The success condition is that the Working Group doesn't need us afterwards. We'd rather they feel like co-authors. Don't over-engineer it.

**Where AI is not the right answer for organisations like 100x.** A trust question - he wants to know we'll tell him not to. Be specific: venture selection judgement and relationship work, where accountability and trust can't be delegated; anywhere the data foundation is broken, because AI on bad data industrialises the mess; low-volume processes where automation costs more than the manual task; knowledge that lives in relationships rather than documents; and anywhere sending venture data to third-party models creates governance or data-protection risk out of proportion to the payoff. The line: our job is to find the few places AI genuinely frees people up, not to maximise AI. That's the human-in-the-loop thread from GatBot and the workshops.

A real example of advising a client *against* an AI use case would be the strongest possible version of this answer - but only if we have a true one. SFCT does not supply it: that engagement ended because their AI budget tightened and they built in-house on Azure, not because we recommended against the use case. What is honestly claimable is the shape of the SFCT work - a cheap prototype built specifically to test the value of a use case before any production investment, which is how you kill things before they get expensive. If either of us can think of a genuine instance where we told a client no, bring it; otherwise use the SFCT framing as it actually happened. Don't manufacture a recommendation-against, because one follow-up question unravels it, and this is the question where we can least afford that.

**If the budget went up £10k.** Don't grab, and don't spread it thinner across the review - convert findings into working things. More prototype-spike time, or implementing the single top quick-win so the engagement ends with something running, plus a couple of staff capability sessions so the handover sticks. Both stay consistent with hand-over-not-dependency, and this also answers his unspoken question about the transition to a build phase without us having to raise it. Avoid "more interviews, more analysis" - it sounds like scope inflation.

## Tandem questions

**Describe a project where your main role was organisational strategy rather than development.** The most important question and our one soft spot, so answer it honestly - the honest version is stronger. Lead with OZEAON, where Jack now leads scope definition, phasing and delivery: running standups and the delivery cadence, writing the scope documents, making the prioritisation calls with the team and confirming direction with the founder. The Phase 0-to-6 structure is the evidence - an enormous product ambition cut into a defensible MVP under a hard 30-45 day window, with follows, mobile responsive, payments and the rest deferred into later phases. That is ruthless prioritisation under constraint, which is the muscle this engagement needs. Several of those calls were data-structure calls, which is the same discipline the brief asks for.

Three genuine secondary examples if he wants more: the OZEAON database audit, where the role was assessment and written recommendations before any remediation; the SFCT helpdesk, which was a strategic question about whether to invest, answered with a cheap prototype; and the ten-workshop programme, which was pure advisory and capability work with no code in it. Use these to widen the answer, not to replace the live OZEAON evidence, which is stronger and more current.

Guardrails so it doesn't tip into overclaim, because Nick will smell the difference. This is release and delivery scoping for a product build, not an organisational data-and-AI strategy engagement - same underlying discipline, not identical work, so frame it as "this is how I think and make prioritisation calls," not "I've done a data and AI review before." Be careful with the word "strategy" itself: what this proves is sound scoping and prioritisation under constraint and holding a delivery line, which is exactly what he's testing for. Claim that, not the bigger word. A confident "we're not McKinsey, and here's why that's what you want" beats pretending to be something we're not - every recommendation comes with a cost and effort estimate we can stand behind because we build these systems the rest of the week.

The strongest single line: on OZEAON we're designing a data model from scratch, where we control it; on the SSC and audit work we inherited and rebuilt an estate we didn't build. We've now worked data structure from both ends. On 100x we'll be assessing and mapping an estate we didn't build and largely can't rebuild - same discipline, applied from the opposite direction. Naming that difference ourselves is more convincing than glossing it.

On people management: worth claiming as delivery leadership - running the cadence, coordinating the people on the project, keeping things moving - but for a two-person team don't headline it in a way that implies a scale of operation we don't run. It supports the workload-shift question more than the strategy one.

**How would you identify and prioritise prototyping opportunities.** Max leads. Criteria, not vibes: a spike qualifies when it de-risks a priority recommendation, is testable in one or two days against real 100x data, and is decision-changing - if the spike fails, the roadmap changes. Prioritise by value-if-true against cost-to-test. Anything that fails those tests is a demo, not a spike, and we don't do demos.

**As a two-person team, how would you manage workload if priorities shifted significantly.** Don't be defensive - it's in the proposal as an honest risk with a mitigation. The fixed price is 100x's flexibility: reprioritisation happens within the envelope at phase boundaries, with explicit trade-offs, so if X comes in we name what goes out rather than pretending to elastic capacity. Both of us work across both strands so nothing is trapped in one head, everything is documented continuously, and the subcontractor network covers genuine specialist gaps such as deep Microsoft 365 governance. Be honest about the limit: we can't absorb a doubled scope, and saying so is the answer he's looking for. The trust-building note - two people means less bench, but also no coordination overhead and total continuity, because the people who start the work finish it.

## Prep actions before the session

Agree who leads which question, per the split above.

Prepare five stories as 60-second versions each: OZEAON (both the current scoping work and the database audit), the UK Skills Explorer, GatBot, the SFCT helpdesk, and the workshop programme. Every answer above maps to one of them - don't improvise the details live.

Do the homework on 100x's current shape - portfolio size, intake rhythm, partner network, any published impact reporting - and reference it specifically, because it shows we did the reading and it's the scaling-pipeline pressure our proposal already identifies. One caution: get these numbers from 100ximpact.org or from your contact, not from any second-hand summary. Figures that have been passed along without a source have a habit of being stale, and being corrected on their own portfolio by the person who runs it costs more than saying nothing at all.

Ten minutes on Nick's background before the session, to calibrate how technical to pitch the answers. Note there's no confusion to resolve about who's who - Nick Stanhope is the Chief Insights Officer and our contact, Kieron Boyle is the CEO.

One more call to your contact at 100x: what is Nick actually deciding between at this stage, and is there a specific worry about us - two-person capacity being the obvious one - so we can address it before he raises it.

Confirm the date and time of the session and put the prep in the diary against it.
