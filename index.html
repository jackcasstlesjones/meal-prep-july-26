Data + knowledge management + AI, the triangle

The ITQ says it that way repeatedly, and our proposal put it on the page: "the interdependencies between data, knowledge management and AI are where most of the value in this brief sits." Everyone else will pitch data + AI. Say the triangle out loud, and mean it, KM is the leg most of the room forgets.

Many of the highest-value AI use cases in orgs like 100x are knowledge-management use cases, retrieval, institutional memory, onboarding. The two assessments inform each other, which is why we treat them as one system from the analysis stage, not two parallel workstreams.

And this isn't abstract for us right now. Jack currently owns OZEAON's KM policy as project manager, inherited from a shambolic starting point, being audited and restructured live. That's the same shape as the KM leg of the 100x brief, an inherited mess getting a considered pass, and it's happening this month. Worth naming explicitly, especially under the WG, AI-limits or prototyping questions, or if he probes what "KM" looks like in practice for us.

General questions

What do you think is the biggest risk in this project that isn't explicitly mentioned in the brief?

The report-to-implementation gap, a great report nobody owns once we leave. That's why Phase 3 makes the Working Group future governance, not audience.

Don't say stakeholder availability, it's already in our proposal and it's what everyone will say. The real risk is the report-to-implementation gap: a small team under growth pressure treats this as a systems project, and it ends up owned by nobody once we leave. A good report that doesn't change how people work.

That's why Phase 3 embeds the Working Group as the future governance body rather than an audience.

Hold that with conviction, and keep the LSE systems boundary in reserve, if LSE's IT environment constrains architecture and AI tooling choices, part of the roadmap could be undeliverable, which is why we probe it in month one, not month five. The reserve option doubles as pre-empting the LSE question.

At the end of six months, what decisions should the 100x senior team be able to make that they can't make today?

Today the build-or-buy, AI-sanction, and data-ownership calls are guesses made in people's heads. In six months they are decisions with evidence and price tags attached, and that means less time wrestling systems, more time on the portfolio.

Answer in their language:

  • whether to build or buy, and roughly what it costs;
  • which AI uses to sanction and which to prohibit, and on what governance basis;
  • who owns which data and what the stewardship model is;
  • what capability to hire or grow internally.

How do you propose working with the 100x Data & AI Working Group?

Co-analysts, not audience, involved from inception, holding the governance mandate at the end. Success is they don't need us afterwards.

Concretely, phase by phase:

  • Kick-off, the WG co-sets the questions the review has to answer, and helps identify the stakeholders we interview together. A WG member sits in on the first couple of interviews so the analysis starts collaborative, not delivered.
  • Analysis, on a fortnightly cadence, draft findings circulated 48 hours ahead so the WG can push back before anything gets locked in. They test the finding while it's still cheap to change.
  • Prototype spikes in Phase 3, each spike has a named WG owner who commissions it, sees the output, and calls it live or dead. That is where the future-governance muscle actually gets built, not from a handover deck.
  • Between sessions, shared workspace so WG members can flag things async, raise concerns, or point us at material we'd otherwise miss.
  • Closing session, the governance mandate is formally handed to the WG, not to us and not to a report on a shelf. They own the follow-on decisions from day one after we leave.

The methodology underneath all of it is bottom-up, findings are built from what the team and ventures actually need, drawn out through conversation, not a template we've dropped in. That's the cleanest counter to "consultants with a slide deck". The success condition is that the WG doesn't need us afterwards, they feel like co-authors, not recipients.

His favourite theme, so bring substance not abstractions. Pick the specifics that fit the flow of his questions rather than reciting all five in a row, which would land as exactly the process-diagram consultancy we're trying to avoid.

Where do you think AI is not the right answer for organisations like 100x?

Our job is to find the few places AI genuinely frees people up, not to maximise AI. Human-in-the-loop is the default, not the exception.

A trust question, he wants to know we'll tell him not to. Ground it in live evidence before the abstractions: GatBot and the workshops are where we've held the human-in-the-loop line in practice; on Amagi Brain Health we had to seriously curb the client's plan for AI usage because AI is not magic, we scoped it back to what would actually work rather than what sounded good on the slide; and CHIRP is shaped deliberately as a research project with Alan Turing Institute data scientists, not a development one, which is exactly the shape of thinking this question is asking about.

From there, be specific about where AI isn't the answer:

  • actual decision-making, AI is for facilitation, structuring the options, surfacing the pattern, never for the call itself;
  • venture selection judgement and relationship work, where accountability and trust can't be delegated;
  • applicant-facing AI in the intake pipeline, the best founders won't want to feel screened by a model rather than read by a person, so heavy AI at the applicant layer risks hurting the very thing 100x is trying to attract;
  • large-dataset analysis where you throw the whole set at a model, context limits mean it silently drops or hallucinates over what won't fit, so the right move is deterministic analysis first and AI on the summarised output, not the raw pile;
  • anywhere the data foundation is broken, because AI on bad data industrialises the mess;
  • low-volume processes where automation costs more than the manual task;
  • knowledge that lives in relationships rather than documents;
  • anywhere sending venture data to third-party models creates governance or data-protection risk out of proportion to the payoff.

A real example of advising a client against an AI use case would be the strongest version, but only if we have a true one. SFCT does not supply it: that engagement ended because their AI budget tightened and they built in-house on Azure, not because we recommended against the use case.

What is honestly claimable is the shape of the SFCT work, a cheap prototype built specifically to test the value of a use case before any production investment, which is how you kill things before they get expensive.

If either of us has a genuine "we told a client no" story, bring it. Otherwise use the SFCT framing as it actually happened. Don't manufacture a recommendation-against, one follow-up unravels it, and this is the question where we can least afford that.

If the budget was increased by £10,000, what would you suggest adding to the scope?

Take a proven spike through to a production tool, so the engagement ends with something running, not a slide.

Convert findings into working things. Take a Phase 3 prototype spike that has already proved its value and carry it through to a usable tool the team actually uses, plus a couple of capability sessions so the handover sticks.

That also answers his unspoken question about the transition to a build phase without us having to raise it, and stays consistent with hand-over-not-dependency.

The proposal already commits time-boxed prototype spikes inside the £28.5k (Phase 3). Frame the £10k as extending a proven spike through to production, not as buying spike time we already promised. Otherwise Nick reads it as us re-selling the same line.

Avoid "more interviews, more analysis", it sounds like scope inflation.

How would you identify and prioritise prototyping opportunities?

Value-if-true against cost-to-test. If a failed spike doesn't change the roadmap, it's a demo, and we don't do demos.

Criteria, not vibes. A spike qualifies when it:

  • De-risks a priority recommendation,
  • Is testable in one or two days against real 100x data,
  • Is decision-changing, if the spike fails, the roadmap changes.

Prioritise by value-if-true against cost-to-test, and expect two shapes to surface, we'd deliberately run a mix of both:

  • Low-hanging fruit, small, obvious, cheap wins that ship value quickly and build trust in the roadmap;
  • Conceptual tools that could streamline whole processes, higher upside but real risk of over-engineering, so we scope them tight and kill them fast if the spike doesn't hold up.

Finding the balance between the two is the judgement call, and feasibility is one of our strong points, because we build these things the rest of the week we can tell quickly what's a two-day spike and what's a three-month project pretending to be one. Anything that fails those tests is a demo, not a spike, and we don't do demos.

As a two-person team, how would you manage workload across six months if priorities shifted significantly?

Fixed price is your flexibility. If X comes in, we name what goes out, we don't pretend to elastic capacity, and we can't absorb a doubled scope.

Don't be defensive, it's in the proposal as an honest risk with a mitigation.

The fixed price is 100x's flexibility: reprioritisation happens within the envelope at phase boundaries, with explicit trade-offs, if X comes in, we name what goes out rather than pretending to elastic capacity.

Key-person risk is named openly in the proposal with the mitigation attached, so this is honest-with-a-plan rather than defensive: both of us work across both strands so nothing is trapped in one head, and everything is documented continuously as we go. Say it in that order, the risk first, the mitigation second, because he'll trust the answer more if we named the concern before he did.

Two of the reasons a two-person shop actually copes with shifting priorities:

  • We work genuinely agile, no committee, no change-request theatre, we can re-plan a phase inside a day rather than a week;
  • Tight-knit talent network we can pull in on demand, business analysts, consultants, data architects, data scientists, technical writers, plus deeper specialists like Microsoft 365 governance, so if a shift needs a skill we don't lead with, we can call it in without hiring or handing the relationship off.

Be honest about the limit: we can't absorb a doubled scope, and saying so is the answer he's looking for.

Two people means less bench, but also no coordination overhead and total continuity, the people who start the work finish it.

Questions

Staged by strength, three to lead with, more loaded in reserve, one personal at the end. A conversation format means he's watching whether we probe.

The strongest three, if you only ask three

What triggered this review now, and why this shape of engagement?

Especially worth asking given he doesn't appear in the 2024 team list, so the Chief Insights Officer role may be recent and his arrival may itself be the trigger. It's a genuine question and it gets him talking about what he's trying to change.

What would you want to be true at month twelve, beyond the report?

This is the one that invites him to raise the build phase himself rather than us fishing for it. It also tells us whether he's thinking implementation or just wants the analysis.

What authority does the Data & AI Working Group actually have, can it decide things, or does it recommend upward?

Practical for delivery, and it signals we're already thinking about who owns this after we leave, which is his register exactly.

Others worth having ready

Where does the boundary with LSE actually sit in practice, IT, security, procurement?

This kills two birds, it resolves the one genuine unknown in our proposal and it shows we understood their setup well enough to spot it. Ask it as a practical scoping question, not a risk flag.

When you imagine this going wrong, what does it look like?

Better than asking what success looks like, which he'll have answered a hundred times. It surfaces his real anxiety, and whatever he says is something we can address on the spot.

How much of this is about the team's own efficiency versus what the ventures experience?

Ventures-first is his thesis, so this lets him talk about the thing he cares about, and the answer genuinely changes where we'd put the depth.

Has anything already been tried internally, any mapping, any tooling someone's built, any AI that's crept in?

Useful for real, and it gently opens the informal-AI-use question that our proposal says needs a non-judgemental framing.

Are there decisions currently on hold waiting for this?

Tells us what the actual urgency is and where the review has to land to be worth the money.

Who are the Working Group and what expertise do they have?

Composition sits alongside the authority question, useful for understanding who we'll be co-analysing with, and where the gaps we need to fill sit.

One personal one

What drew you to 100x, or what does the Chief Insights role cover?

A fit conversation runs both ways, and asking him something human near the end is more natural than another process question. Judge it in the room.
